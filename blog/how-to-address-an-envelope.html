1. Envelope components 101

The USPS and most national posts expect four core lines: sender (return), recipient name, delivery address, and locality line. Optional elements include unit/suite numbers and attention lines.

  • Name line: Full legal name or company, no punctuation.
  • Delivery line: Street + unit (e.g., 455 MARKET ST STE 800).
  • Locality line: CITY STATE ZIP in uppercase; other countries rearrange but the principle stays.
  • Country line: Required whenever sending internationally—write the destination country in English, uppercase.

2. Country-specific line formats

Different posts tweak punctuation, casing, and postal-code placement. Use these quick references before handwriting or printing labels:

United States

Format: CITY STATE ZIP (ZIP+4 allowed). Keep everything uppercase and avoid commas. Example:
SAN FRANCISCO CA 94105-1234.

Canada

Format: CITY PROVINCE POSTAL (ANA NAN). Insert a single space in the postal code and capitalize all letters.

United Kingdom

Post town and postcode each sit on their own line, both uppercase. Example:
LONDON
SW1A 1AA.

Australia

Locality, state abbreviation, and 4-digit postcode share one line: SYDNEY NSW 2000. Write unit numbers before the street (e.g., 5/10 George St).

Mailing elsewhere? Always put the destination country on the last line in English (FRANCE, JAPAN, etc.).

3. Placement & safe zones

USPS automation expects the recipient block between 5/8" and 2 3/4" from the bottom edge. Keep the return address in the top-left within a 2" x 4" rectangle and leave room for a 1.5" x 1.1" stamp box on the top-right.

International posts follow similar logic: recipient centered slightly low, sender top-left, postage top-right. Avoid placing text over artwork or textures that could confuse OCR.

4. Mistakes to avoid

  • Mixing punctuation and lowercase letters in USPS localities—machines prefer uppercase sans punctuation.
  • Forgetting the destination country on international mail.
  • Letting the address drift outside the safe zone, causing smudged postmarks or unreadable scans.
  • Using decorative fonts that collapse at small sizes.
